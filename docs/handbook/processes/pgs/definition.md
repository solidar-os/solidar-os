# Process Definition: Participatory Guarantee Systems (PGS)

**Participatory Guarantee Systems (PGS)** are community-managed quality assurance processes. They certify producers based on the active participation of stakeholders—producers, consumers, and technicians—and are built on a foundation of trust, social networks, and knowledge exchange.

## 1. Core Purpose
The PGS process is designed to transform "social trust" into "verified data." Its primary goal is to ensure that every actor within a solidarity economy network is accountable to the community's shared values, specifically regarding:

* **Producer Integrity:** Verifying the social, environmental, and labor practices of the entity.
* **Goods Quality:** Ensuring that products meet the ethical and ecological standards agreed upon by the network.
* **Source Price Transparency:** Validating the price at the production gate (**Source Price**) to prevent speculation and ensure fair compensation for the producer.

## 2. The Triple Validation Framework
The PGS process provides a rigorous check on three distinct layers of the supply chain:

### A. Producer Validation (Who)
* **Focus:** Labor conditions, environmental stewardship, and territorial roots.
* **Logic:** A supplier is not just a commercial vendor but a project partner. Their identity and methods must be verified through on-site peer reviews conducted by the community.

### B. Goods Validation (What)
* **Focus:** Origin of raw materials, production methods, and seasonality.
* **Logic:** Every item in a catalog must be linked to a specific production protocol. Validation ensures that the goods are consistent with the producer's stated ethics and the community's standards.

### C. Price Validation (How Much)
* **Focus:** Transparency of the cost structure and absence of exploitation.
* **Logic:** The community reviews the producer's internal costs to verify that the **Source Price** (the price paid to the farmer at the farm gate) is fair and sustainable. This price serves as the verified base for any subsequent logistics or distribution markups.

## 3. Accountability vs. Legal Compliance
It is critical to distinguish between these two layers of verification:

| Dimension | Legal Compliance | Internal Accountability (PGS Process) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Authority** | State / Regulatory Bodies | Community / Peer Network |
| **Focus** | Tax, VAT, Legal Status | Ethics, Fair Price, Quality |
| **Mechanism** | Official Audits / Paperwork | Site Visits & Peer Review |
| **Objective** | External Legal Safety | Internal Mutual Trust & Integrity |

## 4. Operational Outcomes
A successful PGS process results in several key outputs for the community:

1.  **Accountability Reports:** Formalized summaries of site visits, peer observations, and consensus-based decisions regarding a supplier's status.
2.  **Verified Source Prices:** A registry of production-gate prices that have been audited and approved as fair by the community committee.
3.  **Trust Assets:** Indicators (such as badges or transparency labels) that communicate the verified status of a product or producer to the final members of the network.
